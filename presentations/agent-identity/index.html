    Agent identity

    Who's calling?

    Identity for the age of agents. Why autonomous agents are a new kind of principal, and why that means rethinking our auth protocols.

    The question

    You hand an AI agent one job: "book my Sydney conference trip under $2,000." It plans the flights and hotels and calls a travel Booking API to make it happen.

    Now it is about to charge $9,000. Who said it could?
    agent · travel booking api
    POST /bookings fare=BIZ-SYD amount=9000 refundable=false
    # authorized by… the user? the app? the agent?

    Who is calling, on whose behalf, and can they prove it? Hold that for the next 40 minutes; by the end you will have three answers.

    Talk track

    Open with a concrete scene, not theory. You delegate one job to an AI agent: book a work trip under a budget. It plans the trip and calls a travel Booking API. Then it tries to charge nine thousand dollars to the corporate card, far over the budget you set. Ask the room out loud: who authorized this, and could anyone prove it after the fact? This booking agent is the one example we follow for the whole talk. Promise three answers by the close: who is calling, on whose behalf, and can they prove it.

    Part one · the familiar ground

    The world we built auth for.

    Before agents, who was even calling? A person, and the software they signed in to. Let us re-teach that ground quickly, because every assumption starts here.

    Talk track

    Open Part one. Keep it warm and quick: we revisit OAuth not to bore the experts but to surface the assumptions hiding in plain sight. The picture to plant is a person, an app they signed in to, and an API behind it. Everything that breaks later breaks because the caller stops being a person at a browser.

    The world we built auth for

    OAuth's home turf: the app acts as you.

    A human signs in and consents once, and the app gets a token that represents them, their scopes, their standing. The app is a client: a vehicle for the token, not a party in its own right.

    sequenceDiagram autonumber actor U as User participant A as App (client) participant AS as Auth Server participant RS as Resource Server U->>A: use the app A->>AS: redirect to authorize U->>AS: log in and consent AS-->>A: access token A->>RS: call API with token RS-->>A: data

    Authorization-code flow: the user consents, the app carries the token.

    Talk track

    Re-teach the basics kindly for newcomers. Four parties: the user who owns the data, the app acting for them, the authorization server that issues tokens, and the resource server that holds the data. OAuth calls this delegated authorization, but look at the shape: the token represents the user, and the app is a client, a registered vehicle, not a party in its own right. One principal on the wire: you. Hold the word client; the moment an agent is the caller, software stops being a vehicle and becomes a principal.

    The world we built auth for · no human

    No human at all: one back-end calls another.

    Take the human out of that flow and you get OAuth's other half, the pattern we have refined for fifteen years: Service A calls Service B through a gateway that checks a token. With the client-credentials grant the service speaks for itself; proper workload identity (SPIFFE) does it better, with no shared secret to leak.

    • The service proves who it is with a client id and secret (or a certificate).
    • That identity was registered ahead of time, by a developer, at each server.
    • The token it gets is a bearer token: hold it, and you can use it.

    It is tempting to file an agent right here just another service-to-service call. It is not that simple, and the next act is about why.

    Talk track

    Pivot cleanly from the last slide: same world, just with the human taken out. This is the slide people reach for when they say "an agent is just another back-end service." For fifteen years we have built exactly this: one service calls another through a gateway that checks a token, with the caller proving itself by client credentials or, better, workload identity like SPIFFE. Plant the three assumptions out loud: pre-registration, a shared secret or key, and a bearer token. Then make the promise: it is tempting to drop an agent into this picture as just another service-to-service call, and the whole next act is about why that is wrong.

    A short history · how we got here

    But first, a little history.

    Two of those assumptions, dropped signatures and bearer tokens, were deliberate trade-offs back in 2012. Worth two minutes to see why.

    Talk track

    A short interlude, not a lecture. The point of the history is empathy: the 2012 trade-offs were reasonable for their world. Mark the one milestone that matters, the 2.0 jump that dropped signatures and leaned on TLS. Two minutes here earns you the right to say later that the model, not the people, is what has to change.

    A short history

    Plot twist: OAuth 1.0 signed every request.

    Proof-of-possession is not a new idea. In 1.0, each request carried a signature proving you held the key. A stolen token was not enough.

    oauth 1.0 · signed request
    GET /api/photos
    Authorization: OAuth oauth_signature="tR3k…", oauth_nonce=…
    # the signature proves possession of the secret
    Talk track

    Correct a common belief before it trips you up later. People say OAuth never had proof-of-possession. Not true. OAuth 1.0 signed every request with an HMAC. The capability existed and worked. That matters, because the story of OAuth 2.0 is a story of deliberately giving that up, for reasons that made sense at the time.

    A short history

    2.0 traded signatures for TLS and bearer tokens.

    Signing was painful, browser crypto was weak, and certificates were costly. So 2.0 dropped signatures and leaned on the secure channel instead.

    OAuth 1.0

    Sign the message

    Security at the application layer. Every request proves possession. Powerful, but fiddly to implement.

    OAuth 2.0

    Secure the channel

    Security moves to the transport layer (TLS). The token becomes a bearer credential. Simpler to adopt, weaker by default.

    "OAuth 2.0 is a bad protocol. WS-* bad." Eran Hammer, lead editor, on resigning from the spec · 2012

    Talk track

    This is the fork in the road. To get broad adoption, the working group shifted the security burden from signing the message to securing the channel, and shipped bearer tokens. It also got renamed from a protocol to a framework. The lead editor was so unhappy he resigned and called it the road to hell. The TLS bet largely paid off. The bearer-token bet is the one that bites agents.

    A short history

    A stolen access token still works. Nothing binds it to who holds it.

    Intercept the booking API's access token, or find it in a log, and you can book flights and charge the card on the user's account. Fine for a fixed backend; the core liability for an agent that carries tokens across services  ⚑

    Talk track

    A bearer access token has no idea who is holding it. Steal it, copy it, or leak it in a log, and it still authorizes calls to the booking API. We accepted that risk because backends sit behind firewalls with short token lifetimes. Agents break that calm: they are numerous, ephemeral, and they carry tokens across services. This is the assumption that matters most once an agent is the caller.

    A short history

    A framework needs profiles. A protocol does not.

    OAuth 2.0 is a base that only becomes usable once you bolt on extensions and pin the choices with a profile.

    PKCEDPoPPAR RARJARJARM Token ExchangemTLSOIDC FAPIDevice Flow
    • OpenID Connect, the identity layer almost everyone uses, is itself a profile on top of OAuth.
    • "We use OAuth2" is never enough. You must also name the flow, PKCE, the endpoints, the profile.
    • A framework is hard to conformance-test. A protocol is not.
    • Guardrail: OAuth is conformance-tested, but only its profiles (OIDC, FAPI). You test the profile, not bare OAuth.
    Talk track

    This is Dick Hardt's framing from his recent interview. A framework leaves so much open that you cannot meaningfully conformance-test it; a prescriptive protocol you can. That is why two "OAuth-compliant" systems rarely interoperate without a profile and a vendor SDK to hide the gaps. Keep the guardrail handy: an expert will point out OIDC and FAPI have conformance suites. Exactly: you test the profile, not bare OAuth. Flexibility was the feature, and also why it sprawled, and why it cannot simply be patched into something agent-shaped.

    Part two · the new caller

    Now an agent is calling.

    Everything so far assumed software a person registered ahead of time. Agents break that assumption on nearly every axis.

    Talk track

    A breath, and a turn. We have set up the world OAuth was built for and the assumptions it carries. Now we change one thing: the caller is an autonomous agent. Watch how much shifts from that single change.

    Enter agents

    From an app to an agent, the actor changes.

    An app is a client that carries your token. An agent is an actor with its own identity. Agents are not clients.

    acts as you

    App · OAuth client

    "load my dashboard." The token represents you; the app is just a vehicle. One principal, a bearer token, a pre-registered client. This is today's OAuth.

    on your behalf

    Task agent

    "summarize my emails." A tightly bounded job, on your behalf, with its own identity. Two questions: can you do this, and did you consent to the agent doing it?

    toward your goal

    Autonomous agent

    "book my Sydney trip." It plans its own steps and spawns sub-agents. Now an open-ended goal needs an agreed bound, and authority must survive the whole chain.

    Each rung adds a problem the old model cannot answer. The rest of the talk is the answers.

    Talk track

    This is the on-ramp and the map for the whole talk. Start from the thing we just spent two acts on: an app is a client that carries your token, today's OAuth. Climb one rung and a task agent becomes an actor with its own identity, so two questions appear, is the action within your authority, and did you consent to this agent doing it? Even a humble "summarize my emails" assistant crosses that line. Climb again and an autonomous agent sets its own next steps from a broad goal: a fixed scope cannot bound it, so it needs an agreed bound and authority that survives being handed to sub-agents. Each rung adds a problem the old model cannot answer, and the rest of the talk is those answers, delegation, missions, and proof-of-possession among them. Proof-of-possession, by the way, is deliberately not a rung; it is an orthogonal, cross-cutting concern. Our running example, booking a trip, is that hardest, top rung.

    Enter agents

    An agent is not a pre-programmed app.

    It decides what to do, and what to call, in the moment. The developer wrote the goal, not the path.

    • Autonomous: it acts toward a goal without a coded script.
    • Reasons about context: it interprets, plans, and adapts.
    • Picks its tools at runtime: the next call is chosen as the task unfolds.
    • Long-running: tasks can span minutes, hours, or days.
    • Spawns sub-agents: it delegates pieces of the work to others.
    Talk track

    We just climbed from app to autonomous agent; now define that new actor plainly for newcomers. The crucial difference from a normal app is that a developer programmed a traditional app's decisions in advance. An agent makes its decisions at runtime: it chooses tools, chains calls, and can spawn helpers. Hold that picture; it is why a pre-registered, build-time identity model does not fit.

    Enter agents

    Not a microservice with an LLM.

    It is tempting to file an agent under Service A calls Service B. But a model, not code, decides what it does, and that quietly breaks four assumptions the service world was built on.

    declared → interpreted

    Intent

    A service encodes intent in code you can test. An agent interprets a goal at runtime, so the same prompt can act differently each run.

    fixed → explored

    Paths

    A service's branches are baked in. An agent explores tools and APIs to find a path, sometimes one no one coded.

    known → discovered

    Dependencies

    A service integrates with known APIs. An agent discovers tools and authority live; its reach is unknown until it reaches.

    service-to-service → on your behalf

    Delegation

    A service runs with its own account. An agent carries a human's authority down a chain, attributed to every hop.

    Framing after Christian Posta · "The Differences Between Microservices and AI Agents"

    Talk track

    This pays off the promise from the service-to-service slide. The comforting mental model is "an agent is a microservice with an LLM attached," because it lets us reuse two decades of gateways, scopes, and service accounts. But a service that merely calls an LLM still has every path pre-defined in code. A real agent lets the model drive the behaviour, and four assumptions break: intent moves from declared to interpreted, paths from fixed to explored, dependencies from known to discovered, and delegation from service-to-service to acting on a human's behalf. The Codex sudo story lands the paths point: it found a workaround it was never handed, not maliciously, just by exploring further than a person would. Posta's conclusion is our whole thesis: gateways, OAuth scopes, and service accounts stay necessary, but they are no longer sufficient.

    The reframing

    Delegation is not impersonation.

    This is the jump from client to actor. Hand the agent your token and it only acts as you. Give it its own identity and it acts on your behalf, with you still in the picture.

    Impersonation

    Acts as you

    It reuses the user's own access token, just like an OAuth client. Every booking is logged as the user, and the agent inherits everything the user can do, not just travel.

    Delegation

    Acts on your behalf

    It calls with its own identity (on-behalf-of). Both principals are visible, so you can trace, scope, and revoke the agent on its own.

    And your token is not even enough the agent often needs authority you do not have, like charging a corporate card you cannot. As its own principal, it brings its own.

    Talk track

    This distinction is the conceptual key to why agents need their own identity. If the agent just borrows the user's token, every action looks like the user did it, and the agent inherits the user's full reach. Delegation keeps two identities in play: the user who asked, and the agent who acted. That is what makes attribution, least privilege, and revocation possible.

    The reframing

    Human? Workload? Or a new layer?

    H

    Not human

    Give it a human identity and it gets the standing of a person without any of the built-in constraints.

    W

    Not quite a workload

    It runs somewhere, so workload identity fits at first. But agents are bursty, ephemeral, and pause and resume across clusters.

    A

    A new layer

    It behaves like an actor. Agent identity sits on top of workload identity, and can collapse into it inside one trust domain, until it cannot.

    Keep this it sets up the whole industry comparison later.

    Talk track

    This is the conceptual on-ramp, and the strongest single idea to land for newcomers. An agent is not a human and not really a workload. It is a new layer, an actor. Inside one trust domain you own, you can collapse that layer into workload identity. The moment an agent crosses into territory its issuing platform does not reach, the layer becomes its own thing. We will return to exactly this when we compare the industry's options.

    Where it breaks · five ways

    Now, watch it break.

    Same booking agent, five concrete failures. Each one is an assumption from Part one collapsing under a single real call.

    Talk track

    Turn the energy up. We have named the assumptions; now we snap the chain. Five breaks, all on the same booking agent, so the audience never loses the thread. Promise that each break is a specific assumption from Part one failing, not a vague worry. Then go straight into the capability gap.

    Where it breaks · 1 of 5

    It crosses orgs. The trust doesn't.

    Booking the trip touches your company's travel API, the airline's, then a payment processor: three different trust domains. Nothing about the agent carries trust from one into the next.

    OAuth's answer

    Pre-arranged trust

    A client can self-publish its identity now (Client ID Metadata Document) and Cross-App Access can bridge domains, but each server still chooses which identities to trust, usually a pre-arranged list, for an app in a consent flow.

    What agents need

    Trusted on first contact

    An autonomous agent accepted the first time it reaches a resource in a domain you do not control, carrying who it is and whose authority, with no pre-arranged list and no human at a browser.

    Park this self-publishing identity is the right instinct, and OAuth is reaching for it; AAuth takes it all the way, to the agent as a cross-domain actor.

    Talk track

    This is the break plain workload identity cannot fix either, and you have to be fair here because OAuth has moved. With the Client ID Metadata Document draft a client can publish its own identity at a URL and a server can fetch it with no pre-registration, which is genuinely the self-published-identity idea, and Cross-App Access can bridge two paired domains. But notice what is still true: every server decides which of those identities it will actually trust, and in enterprises that becomes a pre-arranged allow-list; what is published is an app in a browser-consent flow, not an autonomous agent carrying delegated authority. What agents need is to be accepted as an actor the first time they reach a resource in a domain you do not control, identity and authority together, with no human at a browser. That is the harder problem AAuth targets; the self-published-identity idea, which OAuth is now reaching for too, is just the first piece of it.

    Where it breaks · 2 of 5

    Something broke. Who did it?

    You said three words. A chain of decisions followed, and one of them charged the card.

    You: "book my trip" → Agent plans → Payment sub-agent → Corporate card → Non-refundable booking

    With only the user's token, you are blamed for a decision you never made, by a system you did not directly control.

    Talk track

    This is the compliance and audit angle. You gave an open-ended instruction; the agent planned the trip, spawned a payment sub-agent, and that sub-agent charged the corporate card for a non-refundable fare. If the only identity in the logs is yours, the whole chain is attributed to you. We need to trace user intent, then agent interpretation, then sub-agent action, as distinct, identified steps.

    Where it breaks · 3 of 5

    The token rides a chain today's OAuth doesn't enforce.

    Hours later, several hops deep, agent → booking API → payment processor, across providers, and no one at a browser to approve anything.

    • OAuth can record the chain (RFC 8693 act claims), but the spec says prior actors are informational only, never enforced.
    • Making it enforceable takes yet another draft (verifiable actor-chains, attenuation tokens), so today a token hops deep and hours old is still accepted, no live check, no human in the loop.

    OAuth's own standard admits it does not enforce the multi-hop chain

    Talk track

    The sharp, citable break. OAuth can carry the delegation chain in RFC 8693 act claims, but the standard itself says those prior actors are informational only, never enforced in an access decision, so writing the chain down changes nothing. The fix does exist, as verifiable actor-chains and attenuation-token drafts, but only as competing proposals, not the shipped standard, which is exactly the patch-sprawl we pivot on next. Layer on the clock: the token is several hops deep and hours past any human session, with no browser to redirect for approval. That is the gap AAuth closes with one continuous cryptographic chain, and with authorization that can pause, wait, and resume instead of assuming a person at a browser.

    Where it breaks · 4 of 5

    Same scope. Very different call.

    A scope is standing permission. It cannot tell a helpful call from a harmful one, and a hijacked agent stays perfectly in scope.

    one scope · two bookings
    scope = "bookings:write" # granted, valid
    book economy SYD $320 refundablefine
    book business SYD $9,000 non-refundabledisaster
    # scope cannot tell these apart

    Park this letting the resource decide at call time is exactly how AAuth answers it later.

    Talk track

    This is the failure the audience already fears: prompt injection. The agent holds a legitimate bookings:write scope, and a poisoned search result or itinerary email makes it book a far more expensive, non-refundable fare that is still inside that scope. Scope is a standing grant; it has no idea about the intent of a single call. And before someone says use Rich Authorization Requests: RAR makes the request more precise, but it is still issued ahead of time; the resource still never sees the actual call. Promise that we come back to this with R3, where the resource gets to weigh in on the specific call.

    Where it breaks · 5 of 5

    A valid identity can take an invalid action.

    Zero Trust worked when identity, device, and session were a good-enough proxy for what a caller would do. For agents, identity stops being a proxy for authority.

    Zero Trust for humans

    Centred on identity

    Who is this?  ·  Is the device healthy?  ·  Does the session look normal?

    Zero Trust for agents

    Centred on delegated authority

    Under whose authority?  ·  What was approved?  ·  What are the delegation bounds?  ·  What is the lineage across sub-agents?  ·  Is it still valid at the commit point?

    "Identity is still necessary, but it is no longer enough. Zero Trust for agents has to center on delegated authority."Karl McGuinness · on Harish Peri's Zero Trust thread

    Talk track

    Two complementary points close out the breaks. Harish Peri: per-step validation against a central identity provider does not scale to asynchronous, multi-hop agent workflows that span hours and cross trust domains. Karl McGuinness goes deeper: identity is no longer a sufficient proxy for authority. A valid identity can still produce an invalid action if the agent has drifted from its approved mission, delegated too broadly, crossed a trust boundary, or reached a commit point it was never authorized to cross. So the questions shift from who, device, session to authority, approval, delegation bounds, lineage, and revalidation right before the action becomes real. For humans, Zero Trust centred on identity; for agents it has to centre on delegated authority.

    Part three · the responses

    So how is the industry answering?

    Two fronts. The standards bodies are extending OAuth, a draft for every break. The platforms are building agent-identity layers of their own. We'll take each, then judge them by one lens.

    Talk track

    Move from problem to landscape, and frame it as two distinct responses. First, the standards world is bolting agent extensions onto OAuth, which is the very next slide. Second, the platforms are each shipping their own agent-identity story, which is the spectrum and the deep dives after it. Stay vendor-neutral; the goal is to orient, not to sell. The single lens that makes the platforms comparable, collapse into workload identity or a layer above, lands two slides from now.

    How the industry is responding · the standards

    OAuth can be stretched to meet every one of these breaks.

    There's an IETF spec for each. That's exactly the problem.

    Talk track

    This is the hinge of the whole talk, and it has to be fair to OAuth or the experts tune out. Concede the strong version first: OAuth can reach every one of these breaks, and there is a real IETF spec for each. Be precise about how far along they are: two already shipped as RFCs, PAR and Rich Authorization Requests, and the rest are still racing as drafts, Cross-App Access, identity-chaining, and the Client ID Metadata Document for cross-domain and registration-free identity, verifiable actor-chains for attribution, attenuation tokens for the enforceable chain, TLS-session-bound tokens for theft. Then turn it: that is a stack of separate, non-converged specs, each with its own claims, endpoints, and pre-arranged trust. You can assemble them, but you have built a protocol out of patches and you still have no authoritative way for the agent to carry its identity and delegation across domains. MCP is the lived proof: it adopted OAuth 2.1, immediately needed dynamic client registration, and still left agents with a different client id per server and no governance. So say the thesis slowly: identity has to become a runtime, provable, governable thing, and the agent a first-class actor. You do not get there by extending OAuth; agents need a protocol of their own.

    How the industry is responding · the platforms

    Sometimes the workload is the agent.

    We called an agent not quite a workload. Inside one trust domain it can be one, and Uber wrote up how: each agent is a Kubernetes workload anchored to SPIRE, in an Agent Registry, with one token service. Three invariants make it hold.

    1

    One-to-one mapping

    One agent ↔ one workload. The token service checks the registry that this agent may run on this workload.

    2

    A registry

    A single source of truth for which agent is which, the Agent Registry above.

    3

    Identity continuity

    Stable across schedule, pause, and resume, anchored in that SPIRE credential.

    The catch all three hold for Uber inside one trust domain. Let the agent roam cross-domain or lose the shared registry, and identity has to become a layer of its own.

    Worked example · Uber, "Solving the Identity Crisis for AI Agents"

    Talk track

    This answers a question the next slides assume: when do you even need a separate agent identity? Often you do not. If one agent maps to one workload, a registry records which is which, and that identity stays continuous as the workload is rescheduled, the agent can just ride its workload identity. Uber's writeup is the clean worked example: agents run as Kubernetes workloads, each anchored to a SPIRE workload credential; an Agent Registry is the source of truth; a central token service mints the agent's token only after checking that this agent is authorized on this workload. It works beautifully because every one of those invariants holds inside Uber's single trust domain. That is the tell for the whole survey that follows: the moment an agent roams into a domain you do not control, or there is no shared registry, the collapse stops working and identity has to become a layer of its own.

    How the industry is responding

    Entra Agent ID: an agent inside Entra.

    A specialized Entra service principal, with rich enterprise identity and on-behalf-of flows already in place.

    • A layer above the running workload, not the workload itself.
    • Single vendor (Microsoft), inside one trust domain.
    • A workload identity (e.g. a K8s service account) is exchanged for the agent's token, but the agent stays its own principal.
    Talk track

    Entra Agent ID gives an agent a first-class enterprise identity by extending Entra: it is a specialized service principal with mature on-behalf-of support. Its strength is also its boundary: it lives inside one vendor's trust domain. Great when your world is Microsoft; a different problem once the agent leaves it. Precise on the federation point: the workload's service-account token does not mint the agent token directly. It authenticates the agent's blueprint (replacing that blueprint's client secret), and the blueprint runs the token exchange that issues the agent's own token.

    How the industry is responding

    Agent Substrate: runtime actor identity.

    Google's actor-sandboxing layer. Actors run in hardened microVM workers, with a registry that keeps their identity stable.

    • Actors are multiplexed into pre-warmed worker pods at runtime.
    • Identity stays continuous across pause, resume, and reschedule.
    • Mainly a runtime and workload identity layer, and still collapsible.
    Talk track

    Substrate solves a different slice: keeping an actor's identity stable as it is scheduled, suspended, and resumed across ephemeral workers. The worker pod has a workload identity, but that tells you nothing about which actor is running. A registry keeps actor identity continuous. It is closer to a fine-grained workload identity than a cross-domain authorization protocol.

    How the industry is responding

    AAuth: a cross-domain auth protocol.

    From Dick Hardt, an author of OAuth 2.0. A layer above that bootstraps from workload identity rather than replacing it.

    • Self-published identity for the agent as an actor, aauth:local@domain, no pre-registration.
    • Works across trust boundaries, with no single central control plane.
    • Designed to coexist with OAuth, not replace it.

    The tell everything before AAuth assumed one control plane in one trust domain.

    Talk track

    AAuth is the one built for the open, cross-domain case. Each agent publishes its own cryptographic identity at a well-known URL, verifiable by anyone, with no portal and no shared secret. That self-published-identity idea is not unique to AAuth; OAuth is reaching for it now with Client ID Metadata Documents. What AAuth adds is the rest of the agent: that identity carries delegated authority across domains, with proof on every call, missions, and an enforceable chain, not just an app registering itself. It does not throw away workload identity; it bootstraps from it. This is the option to spend real time on, because it is the one targeting the hard problem the others assume away.

    Talk track

    A closer look at AAuth

    Every agent gets its own identity.

    A cryptographic identifier per instance, bound to a key, published at a URL the agent controls. No portal, no shared secret.

    agent identity
    id: aauth:travel-bot.7f3c@acme.example
    key: Ed25519 public key, at a well-known URL
    # verifiable by anyone, no pre-registration

    Callback ⚑ these are the client ids that actually travel.

    Talk track

    Pay off the first flag. In OAuth a client id is meaningless outside the server that issued it. An AAuth agent identifier is self-sovereign: a name, a key, and metadata at a URL the agent controls, verifiable by any party. This is what replaces API keys. Each instance has its own identity and key, which is what makes per-instance authorization, revocation, and audit possible.

    A closer look at AAuth

    Every request is signed.

    HTTP Message Signatures on every call. A stolen token is useless without the private key. The agent proves possession each time.

    signed request · RFC 9421
    POST /bookings
    Signature-Input: ("@method" "@authority" "@path" ...)
    Signature: :k8sd…:
    Signature-Key: jwt="eyJ…"

    Callback ⚑ this removes the stolen-access-token problem.

    Talk track

    Pay off the bearer-token flag. Every AAuth request is signed with the agent's key, covering the method, host, and path. Intercept the token and you still cannot use it; you do not hold the key. This is proof-of-possession made the default, not an optional extension you have to remember to bolt on. The thing we gave up in 2012 is back, by design.

    A closer look at AAuth

    The resource gets a say.

    In OAuth the resource server is a passive token reader. In AAuth it signs a resource token describing exactly what it needs.

    Agent calls /bookings → 401 + signed resource token → "here is what I require" → Agent gets authorized

    Because the resource signs the request, an attacker cannot swap in a different resource. It also prevents the confused-deputy problem and gives the resource a voice in every authorization.

    Talk track

    This is the architectural inversion. Normally the resource just verifies a token someone else minted. AAuth flips it: the resource cryptographically states what is being requested, binding its own identity, the agent's key, and the scope into a signed token. That stops confused-deputy attacks and, importantly, gives the resource a say every single time, including the chance to step up requirements later.

    A closer look at AAuth

    The Person Server: the user's broker.

    The user chooses it. The agent only ever talks to its Person Server, which holds the agent-to-person binding and brokers consent outward.

    sequenceDiagram autonumber participant Ag as Agent participant R as Booking API participant PS as Person Server actor U as User Ag->>R: signed request R-->>Ag: 401 + resource token Ag->>PS: present resource token PS->>U: approve this? U-->>PS: yes PS-->>Ag: auth token (user + agent) Ag->>R: signed request + auth token R-->>Ag: 200 OK

    Three-party flow: the booking API asks, the Person Server brokers consent.

    Talk track

    This is the answer to "on whose behalf." The Person Server represents the human. The agent never has to know about every resource's identity system; it only talks to its own trusted broker, which federates outward. The PS holds the one-to-one binding between an agent and the person accountable for it, which is what makes the whole chain attributable.

    Beyond the demo · governance

    A mission is context, not authorization.

    A natural-language, user-approved, hash-bound statement of intent, like book my Sydney trip under $2,000, propose before booking, checked against a running mission log.

    "A mission is not an authorization. It is just an agreed-upon context."Dick Hardt

    • The agent identity sets the ceiling; the mission narrows it at runtime.
    • Scopes and policy still authorize. The mission is an additional contextual gate.
    • Per-call context can narrow what an agent does, never widen it.
    Talk track

    State this precisely so you do not overclaim. A mission is the user-approved intent, like "plan and book a trip under this budget, propose before booking." It is immutable, hash-bound, and the Person Server checks each request against it. But say Hardt's line verbatim: a mission is not the authorization. Scopes and policy still decide access; the mission is an extra gate that can only narrow, never widen.

    Beyond the demo · governance

    The resource decides at call time.

    Some operations are granted outright; others are conditional and get re-checked against the actual parameters of the specific call.

    R3 · per-call decision
    quote fare SYD economygranted, proceed
    charge $9,000 non-refundablestep up: confirm
    # a proposal bound to these exact parameters (amount, fare, card)

    Callback this is the answer to break four: intent versus scope, and prompt injection.

    Talk track

    Close the loop you opened in Act 4. Rich Resource Requests let a resource mark some operations as conditional. When the agent invokes one, the booking API builds a proposal bound to the concrete parameters, how large the charge is, which fare, which card, and that gets re-evaluated before it runs. An approval to book a $320 fare cannot be replayed to charge $9,000. This is the structural answer to prompt injection. Note honestly: R3 is exploratory, with no implementation yet.

    Beyond the demo · adoption

    A sibling protocol, not a replacement.

    You adopt it one mode at a time, and it wraps what you already run. Your read-only flight-search API keeps its OAuth; only the booking-and-charge path needs the full flow.

    • A drop-in for API keys (identity mode) and for OAuth (resource-managed mode).
    • It wraps existing OAuth and OIDC; the four modes are additive.
    • Existing OAuth clients stay put; AAuth onboards a new class of agentic actors.

    "OAuth works great where it is deployed. Why break what is working?"Dick Hardt

    Talk track

    Pre-empt the obvious question: do we throw away OAuth? No. This is coexistence, not migration. AAuth deliberately uses its own header for challenges so it can run side by side with OAuth on the same server. The resource-managed mode lets AAuth agents use a plain OAuth resource. Existing clients keep working; agents get a protocol built for them. Use Hardt's line to land it.

    An honest status check.

    Hold the enthusiasm at the right level. This is a direction, not a finished standard.

    • An open debate: is durable mission governance a protocol primitive, or a layer above?
    • It coexists with OAuth, by design, rather than replacing it.
